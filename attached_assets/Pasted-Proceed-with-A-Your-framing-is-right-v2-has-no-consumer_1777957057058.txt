Your framing is right. v2 has no consumers, the in-flight fixes are subsumed by the 7-phase design, and the architect's three flags (synthetic handoff, silent-default derivedFrom, unenforced consumed-handoff link) all collapse into the new design's existing primitives — write-time directive enforcement, frozen L1 evidence pointer, per-track evidence blocks. The architect's concerns aren't lost in the pivot, they get solved by stronger primitives instead of patches. Two migrations versus one is the deciding economic argument, and pivoting before consumers exist is exactly when the cost is lowest.
One framing call: keep it as v2.0.0, not v3.0.0. v1 is what's deployed; v2 hasn't shipped; the version number is still moldable. Burning v2.0.0 on a half-design and saving v3.0.0 for "the real one" wastes a major version and tells future readers the v2 effort was a false start when it isn't — it's the same effort, redesigned mid-flight before any consumer touched it.
Adopting all five of your refinements. Each belongs in the schema, with one constraint per:

notApplicable: { reason, evidenceCited } as a typed peer of consumedDirective — yes. The mAb-CYP example generalizes: validators should accept a fixture catalog of (compoundClass, predictionDomain) → notApplicable so categorical non-applicability is satisfied automatically rather than requiring a fabricated directive. Worth its own type — CategoricalExemption — referenced by notApplicable.reason so the audit trail shows the exemption wasn't a one-off judgment call.
gatePolicy embedded in the ConfirmationPackage — yes, and this is what makes the package actually portable across partners. Without it, partner B's Phase1Planner is reading numbers that passed an unknown gate. The embedded policy is a snapshot at gate time, immutable, with its own version tag so policy evolution is itself auditable.
Typed tolerance: { kind, value, unit } — yes, and kind should be a closed enum (absolute | log10 | fold | percentRelative), not extensible at the type level. New tolerance kinds need explicit schema migration, not silent expansion at the integration layer.
l1EvidenceCited: { pointer, fields: string[] } on overrides — yes. This is the regulatory-grade move and worth holding the line on.
repairPath on partial joint outputs — yes, with blocksWhichDownstreamDecisions as you have it. This stops partial from becoming a graveyard.

And your sixth (typed FalsifierId on override) — yes, and make it a typed reference to a falsifier registry rather than a string. The registry is itself versioned, so MABEL-bias logic in Phase1Planner branches on a stable identifier, not on the prose someone wrote in the falsifier description that day.
One thing I'd add. Override blocks need an authority chain on the actor side, mirroring your point 4 on the evidence side. Today an override carries {actor, reason, falsifierAccepted, l1EvidenceCited}. Expand to {actor, role, authorityCitation: PolicyRef, reason, falsifierAccepted, l1EvidenceCited}. "Bob accepted" doesn't survive an FDA inspector; "Bob in role X under policy Y accepted, citing fields A, B, C of evidence Z" does. Same standard you're already applying to evidence, applied symmetrically to authority.
Pivot sequencing. The risk in pivot is scope sprawl. Sub-milestones, in order, and resist parallelizing them even though no consumer is blocking:
First, lock the schema — typed states, evidence blocks, gatePolicy, tolerance, repairPath, override authority chain, falsifier registry, categorical exemption catalog. Schema alone, no validator yet. This is where alignment matters most and where churn is cheapest.
Second, write the validators. The write-time enforcement is what makes the anti-fabrication properties real. Validators come second because they're meaningless without locked types but must ship before any producer code lands, otherwise producers fabricate against an unenforced contract and refactor pain compounds.
Third, migrate derivePhase1Defaults to read per-layer confidence and the new joint-output blocks. This is the breaking change you flagged earlier — its signature change is where the actual refactor pain lives, not in the type definitions.
Fourth, Phase1Planner consumer changes — MABEL-bias logic branching on accepted FalsifierId, IND-gate reading gatePolicy from the package, override-authority-chain audit display.
Coordination cost. Whoever's mid-flight on the unblinded-run fix needs an explicit "stop, design pivoted" message and a pointer to the new spec — not just a Slack ping. Write the pivot decision down for the team, for the architect who flagged the original v2 issues, and for you two weeks from now when context decay sets in. The 7-phase Confirmation pipeline we just sketched is the natural artifact.
