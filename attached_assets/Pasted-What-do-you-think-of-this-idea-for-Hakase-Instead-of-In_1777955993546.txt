This way we can collaborate with other companies with what we haveClaude responded: The collaboration angle is the strongest argument here — fewer external gates means a cleaner story when you're plugging partners into the pipeline, and "In-Vi…The collaboration angle is the strongest argument here — fewer external gates means a cleaner story when you're plugging partners into the pipeline, and "In-Vitro → Confirmation → Animal → Phase 1" reads much more naturally to a BD audience than four discrete layers. So as a partner-facing architecture, I think merging In-Silico and Ex-Vivo into one "Confirmation Layer" is a real upgrade.
The thing I'd push back on is collapsing them internally. In-silico runs in hours to days at near-zero marginal cost; ex-vivo runs in weeks at meaningful cost. If you treat them as one layer with one gate, you lose the ability to fail fast on in-silico before you commit ex-vivo budget — which is exactly the kind of waste the layered structure was supposed to prevent. The healthier pattern is probably: one external layer (L2 Confirmation) with two parallel sub-tracks that have asymmetric timing and an internal sequencing rule — in-silico runs first or alongside, ex-vivo only commits once in-silico predictions cross a threshold. Partners see one waypoint; the curators inside still have two gates.
The "Phase 1 based on inputs from all 3 layers" part is good and worth making explicit in the typed contract. Right now the PreClinicalPackage that L4 hands to Phase1Planner carries provenance — under the new shape, Phase 1 should be receiving an aggregated package where each upstream layer's evidence, overrides, and confidence are first-class fields, not just a flattened summary from the last layer. That keeps the override/provenance pattern you established intact across the restructure.
